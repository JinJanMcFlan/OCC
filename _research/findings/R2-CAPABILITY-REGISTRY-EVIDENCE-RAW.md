OCC Research Run R2 — Capability Registry EvidenceFounder BriefThe Overseer Command Center (OCC) is predicated on the hypothesis that complex, multi-part workflows can be deterministically routed across a heterogeneous landscape of artificial intelligence resources. However, the foundational challenge of any provider-neutral orchestration layer is environmental volatility. The underlying resources—cloud-based models, local execution environments, third-party APIs, and external toolchains—are constantly fluctuating. Context limits expand, pricing structures shift, latency spikes during peak hours, and models undergo silent degradation or unannounced deprecation. If OCC relies on static configuration files, hardcoded leaderboards, or anecdotal assumptions about vendor capabilities, its routing decisions will rapidly become inefficient, unsafe, or entirely non-functional.To address this, OCC requires a dynamic, evidence-backed knowledge base: the Capability Registry. This research run defines the exact architectural requirements for maintaining durable, verifiable information about every available capability. The central objective is to transition OCC from making routing guesses based on marketing claims to making deterministic routing decisions based on continuous empirical evidence.The investigation into robust adjacent domains—spanning distributed compute scheduling, military intelligence evaluation, microservice observability, and safety engineering—reveals that the registry cannot function as a passive directory. Historical attempts at centralized service directories, such as the UDDI registries of the early 2000s, ultimately failed due to their reliance on manual updates and their vulnerability to category drift and stale records. To avoid this fate, the OCC Capability Registry must operate as a continuous observability platform. It must actively ingest outcome feedback, perform health checks, and validate capabilities through direct execution telemetry.A critical design requirement is the prevention of self-deception. OCC must rigorously separate the source of a claim from the credibility of the data. By adopting a dual-axis confidence matrix inspired by the NATO Admiralty System, OCC can systematically track when a historically reliable provider delivers an improbable or hallucinated result. This ensures that contradictory evidence is preserved and evaluated, rather than allowing a single failed run to permanently blacklist a capability or a single lucky success to artificially inflate its reliability. Furthermore, the integration of semantic circuit breaker patterns—adapting concepts from network resilience frameworks—enables OCC to gracefully degrade routing choices and trigger dynamic fallbacks when a capability exhibits operational instability or output degradation.The integration between the task facts extracted in R1 and the evidence maintained in the Capability Registry forms the core of OCC’s routing engine. This matchmaking process must strictly adhere to the five locked routing factors: Cost, Time urgency, User presence, Model fit, and Required output quality. Operating similarly to the Kubernetes scheduling framework, OCC must first apply hard constraints—such as strict privacy boundaries or mandatory filesystem access—as binary filters. Only the capabilities that survive these non-negotiable gates are subjected to weighted scoring against the five factors.Ultimately, the Capability Registry is not a vendor ranking system; it is a defensive survival layer. It abstracts the chaotic, rapidly shifting AI landscape into structured, machine-readable metrics. By implementing a rigorous evidence ladder, resilient circuit breaking, and explainable decision logging, OCC will achieve an auto-delegation architecture capable of sustaining reliable operations regardless of how the underlying provider ecosystem evolves.Definition of the Capability RegistryThe Capability Registry serves as the central intelligence mechanism for OCC, bridging the conceptual gap between what a user's task requires and what the operational environment can reliably execute at any given moment. It is the definitive source of truth regarding the state, economics, and historical performance of the system's resources.What the Capability Registry Is:
The registry is an active, evidence-backed state machine. It tracks the real-time operational status, proven capabilities, and historical reliability of all available agents, models, local machines, and external tools. Functionally, it serves as a continuous observability layer, dynamically ingesting outcome feedback from completed tasks to continuously adjust confidence scores, error rates, and latency expectations. It acts as a deterministic matching engine, providing the exact structured data required by OCC’s hard-rule gates and weighted scoring algorithms to evaluate candidate routes. Furthermore, it enforces a provider-agnostic infrastructure by defining capabilities abstractly—such as "local filesystem write access," "128,000 token context window," or "strict data privacy"—rather than relying on proprietary vendor terminology or specific product names.What the Capability Registry Is Not:
The registry is fundamentally not a static configuration file. It is not a hardcoded manifest of models, endpoints, or prices that requires manual intervention every time a provider updates an API. It is also not a generalized leaderboard or a "best model" ranking system. Because OCC routes tasks contextually based on five distinct factors, a model that is optimal for a complex, non-urgent task may be completely inappropriate for a highly urgent, cost-sensitive task. Finally, the registry is not a mechanism for making absolute mathematical guarantees of success. Given the probabilistic nature of generative models, the registry cannot promise perfect execution; instead, it provides the highest-probability route based on the most robust current evidence.Proposed Minimum Record for Each CapabilityTo make reliable routing decisions, OCC must maintain a highly specific, structured record for each capability. This data must be actively managed, continuously verified, and strictly versioned. To mitigate this, the Capability Registry must implement a rigorous evidence-quality ladder. This methodology adapts the principles of the NATO Admiralty System and evidence quality ladders used in complex systems analysis to mathematically weigh the trustworthiness of its data.The Practical Evidence LadderOCC must classify all capability facts into one of five distinct tiers of evidence strength.Directly Observed (Highest Confidence): Data gathered through OCC's own telemetry during the actual execution of a workload. This represents incontrovertible empirical evidence. Example: OCC's internal OpenTelemetry traces record that a specific local model consistently processes 45 tokens per second under current hardware loads.Independent Testing / Cryptographic Verification: Data sourced from trusted, standardized third-party benchmarks, digital signatures, or deterministic programmatic validation. Example: A capability's output format is continuously validated against strict OpenAPI 3.1 schema definitions using automated schema validators.Officially Claimed: Specifications published by the vendor, API provider, or hardware manufacturer. While useful for establishing baseline routing parameters, this level of evidence is inherently subject to marketing bias, undocumented limitations, and silent degradation. Example: A provider claims a 1-million-token context window.Inferred: Capabilities assumed based on the historical performance of similar models within the same family, or extrapolated from previous software versions. Example: Inferring that a newly released minor version of an API inherits the exact tool-calling syntax and rate limits of its immediate predecessor.Unknown (Lowest Confidence): Missing fields, unpopulated attributes, or capabilities that have never been tested, documented, or observed by OCC.The Dual-Axis Confidence MatrixTo prevent self-deception and avoid over-indexing on isolated events, OCC must separate the reliability of the source from the credibility of the specific information. Borrowing directly from the NATO Admiralty System, OCC implements a machine-readable dual-axis matrix.Source Reliability (A-F): How historically trustworthy is the entity providing this information? (e.g., A consistently transparent local hardware probe vs. an unverified third-party API wrapper).Information Credibility (1-6): Does this specific claim make logical sense, and has it been corroborated by OCC's own telemetry?Application Example: A highly reputable provider (Source Reliability: B - Usually Reliable) officially claims their new model guarantees flawless JSON output. However, OCC's direct observation during execution shows the model frequently injects conversational markdown, violating the schema. The credibility of the claim drops to 5 (Improbable), creating a B5 rating for that specific capability fact. OCC's router operates on the empirical B5 reality—applying heavy penalties to the model's fitness for structured data tasks—rather than the marketing claim.Freshness Rules and Handling Changing InformationCapability data is highly ephemeral. OCC must establish strict temporal decay rules for evidence to handle the rapid evolution of the AI landscape.Dynamic Attributes (Status, Latency, Operational Limits): These metrics decay rapidly. Evidence older than a few minutes is inherently suspect. OCC should utilize service mesh methodologies, akin to HashiCorp Consul's continuous polling and gossip protocols, to aggressively mark these fields as stale and re-verify them.Economic Attributes (Pricing, Tier Limits): These metrics decay slowly but carry severe financial risk if incorrect. OCC must monitor for HTTP deprecation headers, API versioning updates, or account billing threshold alerts to trigger an immediate automated refresh or flag the user for manual review.Handling Unavailability and Outages: If a local machine becomes physically disconnected or a remote API returns repeated timeout errors, OCC implements a Circuit Breaker Pattern. The capability is instantly marked as "Open" (unavailable) for a designated cooling-off period. During this window, OCC dynamically falls back to the next highest-scoring candidate. Once the sleep window expires, the circuit transitions to "Half-Open," allowing OCC to route a single, lightweight test task to verify recovery before fully restoring the capability's routing privileges.How the Registry Connects to RoutingThe OCC router functions as a sophisticated matchmaking engine, calculating the optimal intersection between the Task Fact Record (extracted during the R1 classification phase) and the empirical data stored in the Capability Registry. This process must be deterministic, highly explainable, and executed in distinct phases. This architecture heavily mirrors the Kubernetes scheduling framework, which strictly separates filtering from scoring, as well as the dynamic ClassAds matchmaking mechanism utilized by HTCondor.Phase 1: Eliminate Options (Hard Restrictions)Before attempting to weigh complex tradeoffs, OCC must ruthlessly prune the candidate pool. Hard restrictions represent non-negotiable binary gates; a capability either meets the requirement or it is immediately disqualified.Example 1 - Privacy Constraints: R1 determines that a task involves analyzing proprietary, unreleased financial data, setting the Task Fact for Privacy to "Strict Local." The router queries the Capability Registry and eliminates all options where the Deployment Topology is not explicitly flagged as local or physically owned-compute.Example 2 - Tool Access: R1 identifies that the task requires modifying files within a specific IDE project directory. The router filters out any models or execution environments that lack verified filesystem write permissions.Example 3 - Task Size: R1 estimates the input context size at 85,000 tokens. The registry filters out all capabilities with a hard Context Limit below 85,000, preventing guaranteed out-of-memory or token-limit execution errors.Phase 2: Compare Remaining Options (Weighted Scoring)The capabilities that survive the hard gates are then scored against the user's configured priority weights for the five locked routing factors: Cost, Time urgency, User presence, Model fit, and Required output quality.Example 4 - Urgency vs. Presence Tradeoffs: R1 indicates the task is "Non-Urgent" (results are not needed for 12 hours) and the User Presence is "Asleep/Autonomous." Because the user is not actively waiting, the scheduler heavily discounts the Time urgency factor. The registry reveals that a highly capable remote model offers a 70% cost discount via a deferred batch-processing API. OCC selects the batch API, maximizing cost efficiency without violating the user's autonomous workflow parameters.Example 5 - Quality vs. Cost: R1 specifies the task requires the "Highest Output Quality Tier" for a complex reasoning problem. The registry indicates that several fast, cheap local models have a low Model Fit and low historical Quality score for deep reasoning. Consequently, the router allows a slower, more expensive, high-tier capability to win the route, as the user's quality weight overrides the cost penalty.Phase 3: Explain the DecisionTo build user trust and facilitate debugging, OCC must generate a clear, auditable trail explaining exactly why a route was selected and why seemingly viable alternatives were rejected. This approach adapts the principles of Goal Structuring Notation (GSN), mapping the final decision explicitly back to the supporting evidence and task requirements.Sample Decision Log: "Route Selected: Local_Owned_Cluster_Node_1. Rationale: R1 Task Fact required strict privacy (Hard Gate). Candidate Remote_API_High_Tier rejected due to remote execution topology. Local_Owned_Cluster_Node_1 selected over Local_Laptop_Environment due to superior cudaGetDeviceProperties VRAM profiling, resulting in a 40% higher Model Fit score and satisfying the Time Urgency parameters."Rules for Handling Unavailable, Stale, Missing, Uncertain, or Contradictory InformationA production-grade orchestration system must be resilient enough to survive imperfect, degrading, or conflicting information without catastrophic failure. OCC handles environmental uncertainty by enforcing strict state management protocols.Unavailable Information (Outages): If a capability goes offline or experiences a severe network partition, the semantic circuit breaker opens. The router immediately disqualifies the option and recalculates scores for the remaining candidates. If no candidates survive the hard gates due to the outage, the task state shifts to "Deferred/Blocked," holding the task in a queue and alerting the user rather than failing silently or hallucinating a response.Stale Information: If critical data—such as dynamic pricing or rate limits—exceeds its Time-to-Live (TTL) and cannot be immediately verified, OCC applies a mathematical penalty to the capability's Confidence Level. If the user's R1 task facts indicate extreme budget sensitivity, OCC may temporarily block routing to capabilities with stale cost data to prevent unexpected financial overages.Missing Information: If an optional capability field is missing, OCC treats it as a null value and scores it conservatively. If a required constraint field is missing (e.g., Privacy and Data Handling), OCC applies a "fail-safe" default. It assumes the most restrictive and hazardous state—for example, assuming an unverified API does retain user data for training unless explicit cryptographic or contractual evidence proves otherwise.Contradictory Information: When official marketing claims clash with observed telemetry, OCC strictly favors Directly Observed evidence. If a provider claims high throughput, but OCC telemetry records severe latency spikes, the Admiralty code for that metric shifts to reflect low information credibility. OCC explicitly preserves the contradiction in the registry audit log, ensuring developers can investigate the discrepancy without allowing the false claim to influence active routing.Outcome Feedback Without Self-DeceptionFor the Capability Registry to adapt to a shifting landscape, OCC must learn safely from the outcomes of completed tasks. However, treating a single anomalous failure as definitive proof of incompetence, or a single lucky run as proof of mastery, will lead to erratic, unstable routing. The feedback loop must be structured, statistically smoothed, and protected against self-deception.What Outcomes Should Be RecordedOCC must systematically log both quantitative performance metrics and qualitative execution results:Quantitative: Actual execution time, time-to-first-token, total token throughput, and actual financial cost incurred (to detect shadow pricing changes or undocumented token overhead).Qualitative: Task success state (as determined by the OCC evaluator), tool execution errors (e.g., syntax errors, hallucinated function calls), and schema validation failures (e.g., failing strict OpenAPI specification checks).Updating Confidence: Automatic vs. Human ReviewAutomatic Updates: Purely quantitative metrics and deterministic errors update the registry automatically using rolling historical averages and exponential decay algorithms to smooth out isolated network jitter. If an API repeatedly returns HTTP 429 (Too Many Requests), OCC automatically adjusts the recorded rate-limit ceiling downward.Human Review Required: Subtle qualitative shifts require human intervention or advanced deep-evaluator review. If a capability repeatedly fails a complex reasoning task it previously excelled at (indicating silent semantic degradation), OCC flags the capability's Model Fit score as uncertain. The system does not automatically revoke a model's overarching "Coding" capability based on one failed script; instead, it incrementally lowers the Confidence Level and surfaces the anomaly to the user for review.Preserving the Evidence TrailTo avoid self-deception, OCC must act similarly to the Medical Priority Dispatch System (MPDS), which relies on structured feedback to refine its protocols without ever losing the history of why a specific decision was made in the moment. If OCC downgrades a capability record based on recent failures, it preserves the original R1 Task Facts, the previous capability state, and the specific execution logs that triggered the downgrade. This continuous safety assurance loop—mirroring the FAA's Safety Management System (SMS) principles—ensures that if a failure was caused by a transient environmental issue rather than a degraded model, the system can eventually self-correct without hiding the root cause.Adjacent Systems and Improvement OpportunitiesThe following table details 15 robust systems, operational fields, and architectural patterns that maintain changing knowledge about dynamic resources and make critical decisions from that data. systems, continuous observability patterns, and the rigid constraints of the OCC architecture, the following conclusions are ready to drive the immediate implementation direction of the Capability Registry.What OCC Cannot Route Reliably WithoutStrict Separation of Hard Gates and Weighted Scoring: OCC must adopt a multi-phased router design. Without a deterministic mechanism to instantly eliminate options that violate privacy, tool constraints, or cost ceilings (Phase 1 Filtering), any attempt at weighted scoring (Phase 2) becomes unsafe and mathematically unstable.Active Telemetry and Semantic Circuit Breakers: A static list of models and prices guarantees catastrophic routing failures in a volatile ecosystem. OCC must actively track uptime, latency, and circuit breaker states (Open/Closed/Half-Open) using continuous health checks.Dual-Axis Evidence Tracking: To prevent self-deception, OCC must decouple official provider claims from observed empirical credibility. Utilizing an Admiralty System-style matrix ensures OCC preserves contradictory evidence and bases routing on reality, not marketing.What Can Wait Until LaterPredictive Latency Simulation: While Google Borg's 60-million parameter Markovian Service Rate predictions are highly efficient for global data centers, implementing complex digital twin simulations for OCC's initial routing engine introduces unnecessary computational overhead. Simple rolling historical averages are sufficient for the first iteration.Decentralized Self-Reporting Capabilities: Allowing remote agents to dynamically self-report their capabilities—akin to PCI Configuration Space enumeration—is a powerful advanced feature. However, initially, the Registry can be safely populated by OCC's internal orchestration logic, baseline provider APIs, and user configuration.What Remains UnprovenSemantic Circuit Breaking at Scale: While network timeouts easily trip traditional microservice circuit breakers, detecting "semantic degradation" (e.g., a model suddenly generating poor code without throwing an HTTP error) and automatically tripping a circuit breaker remains an emerging, unproven area requiring extensive real-world testing.Local Classifier Latency vs. Routing Efficiency: The speed and accuracy of the local classifier responsible for extracting R1 Task Facts remain unknown. If the classification and registry lookup phases take longer than the actual task execution, the orchestration system's efficiency collapses.What Should Feed the Next Routing-Policy Research RunDeterministic Scoring Mathematics: Research must now focus on the exact mathematical functions used in Phase 2 (Weighted Scoring). How are the user's disparate priority weights (Cost, Quality, Time) normalized mathematically against the wildly different data types (dollars, milliseconds, abstract capability tiers) stored in the Registry?Evaluator Design for Outcome Feedback: Because the Capability Registry relies heavily on outcome feedback to self-heal and adjust confidence scores, the next research run must precisely define how the OCC Evaluator determines task success or failure without relying exclusively on expensive, slow "LLM-as-a-judge" patterns.What Evidence is Still Needed Before Any Implementation DecisionStorage Architecture Benchmarks: OCC's current direction assumes JSON/in-memory storage. However, as the Capability Registry expands with continuous telemetry, historical reliability logs, dual-axis confidence matrices, and deep evidence trails, in-memory JSON may face extreme memory bloat and unacceptable serialization times. Comprehensive benchmarks comparing local JSON performance to lightweight SQL or time-series databases are strictly required before locking in the state management storage layer.
