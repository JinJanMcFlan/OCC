OCC Research Run R4: Outcome Feedback and Safe Improvement ArchitectureFounder BriefThe central challenge of orchestrating autonomous artificial intelligence capabilities lies in distinguishing between genuine semantic capability, operational volatility, and noisy evaluation data. If the Overseer Command Center (OCC) updates its routing logic based on a single successful execution, it risks over-indexing on a statistical anomaly. Conversely, if the orchestrator downgrades a capability’s fitness score due to a temporary provider outage, a poorly constructed user prompt, or a flawed secondary model evaluation, the system falls into a cycle of self-deception. The capability routing matrix requires a mathematically sound, evidence-based mechanism to record outcomes and safely update the Capability Registry without overreacting to noise.This analysis establishes a comprehensive framework for safe capability improvement. By examining robust statistical, operational, and safety-critical paradigms outside the immediate sphere of AI orchestration, a highly resilient feedback loop is synthesized. Competitive rating systems such as Glicko-2 and Bayesian Elo offer mechanisms for tracking skill and uncertainty over time. Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) practices, specifically multi-window burn rates and circuit breakers, provide the foundation for separating operational health from cognitive ability. Evidence fusion frameworks like the Dempster-Shafer Theory and random-effects meta-analysis offer mathematical models for resolving contradictory outcomes. Finally, scientific provenance models such as the W3C PROV-DM ensure that every data point injected into the router possesses an immutable lineage.The proposed architecture cleanly separates operational health from semantic capability. A network timeout or rate-limit error represents an operational failure; it triggers a deterministic circuit breaker to route traffic elsewhere temporarily but does not degrade the model's underlying semantic quality rating. Conversely, a technical success that results in a human-rejected output degrades the semantic quality rating but leaves operational health metrics untouched. To manage the inherent uncertainty in subjective quality evaluations, the system adapts the Glicko-2 rating system's concepts of Rating Deviation and Volatility. This allows the capability registry to update confidence progressively over multiple runs, trusting historical consistency over isolated anomalies.Furthermore, to combat the inherent unreliability of "model-as-a-judge" evaluations, the architecture incorporates principles from active learning, specifically Query-by-Committee, alongside robust loss function theory. By treating evaluations as inherently noisy labels, the system ensures that conflicting secondary evaluations escalate to human review rather than silently corrupting the router's decision weights.Ultimately, this analysis defines the minimum viable information the system must record after a task finishes, how those records securely and incrementally update the Capability Registry, and what governance protocols are required to prevent runaway automation from destabilizing the routing matrix. The smallest safe first approach relies on automated operational telemetry combined with human-in-the-loop quality evaluations, deferring fully autonomous capability reassignment until semantic evaluation algorithms prove mathematically robust and free from confirmation bias.Minimum Outcome-Record MapTo safely update routing knowledge, the system must capture the complete context of an execution attempt. Relying merely on a binary success or failure flag is deeply insufficient for an orchestrator managing multiple providers and complex task shapes. The outcome record must separate the technical execution state, the operational cost, the semantic quality of the output, and the exact provenance of the evaluation.To structure this data, the architecture borrows from the W3C PROV-DM (Provenance Data Model), which organizes historical records into Entities, Activities, and Agents. Within this framework, the prompt and the final output are Entities, the inference execution is an Activity, and the specific model or human evaluator is the Agent. A model that generates a perfect response but takes twice as long as expected will see its "Time / Urgency" rating penalized, while its "Output Quality Tier" and "Model Fit" ratings remain strong or even increase.Safe Evidence-Update ModelTranslating recorded outcomes into capability knowledge updates is the most delicate operation in the orchestration lifecycle. The central philosophy is to separate operational metrics, which must update instantly to protect the system, from semantic capabilities, which must update gradually to prevent statistical noise from corrupting the routing logic.Automated Operational UpdatesFacts regarding network health, exact cost, measured latency, and tool availability must influence the router immediately. To manage this safely without degrading capability confidence, the architecture adapts Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) practices, specifically utilizing Error Budgets and the Circuit Breaker pattern.Instead of assuming a model's quality has degraded when an API returns HTTP 500 errors, the system tracks the operational failure against a predefined Error Budget for that specific provider route. The system utilizes a multi-window burn rate alert strategy. For example, comparing a 5-minute fast-burn window (e.g., consuming budget at 14.4x the baseline) against a 1-hour slow-burn window prevents the system from overreacting to a single dropped packet while ensuring immediate response to genuine outages. When the burn rate threshold is exceeded, the Circuit Breaker enters an "Open" state, temporarily rendering the capability ineligible for routing. After a prescribed cooldown, it transitions to a "Half-Open" state to test a single request. If successful, the circuit closes. Throughout this entire operational incident, the model's underlying semantic quality rating in the Capability Registry remains entirely untouched.Gradual Semantic UpdatesEvaluating a model's ability to reason or satisfy complex user goals requires a mathematical model that respects uncertainty. The system adapts the principles of the Glicko-2 rating system, commonly used in competitive domains to track dynamic skill levels. In this framework, each capability maintains three core variables for every distinct task shape: a Rating ($\mu$) representing estimated quality, a Rating Deviation ($\phi$) representing uncertainty, and a Volatility ($\sigma$) representing the expected fluctuation in performance over time.When a semantic outcome is recorded, the magnitude of the registry update is governed by the Rating Deviation. A single successful run from an untested model (high $\phi$) will increase its Rating moderately while decreasing its Deviation, as the system gains evidence. Conversely, a single semantic failure from a historically exceptional model (low $\phi$) will barely dent its Rating, because the mathematical weight of long-term historical evidence outweighs the single anomaly. However, if the historically exceptional model begins failing repeatedly, the Illinois algorithm driving the Volatility ($\sigma$) parameter detects the erratic performance, dynamically increasing the Rating Deviation ($\phi$) and allowing the Rating ($\mu$) to drop more rapidly to reflect the new reality. This solves the problem of overreacting to one failed run while maintaining responsiveness to genuine, sustained model degradation.To further safeguard this process against subtle, slow-moving degradation, the system can deploy Wald's Sequential Probability Ratio Test (SPRT). SPRT calculates the cumulative log-likelihood ratio of successful versus failed outcomes as they arrive. If the cumulative sum crosses a predefined upper or lower mathematical boundary, the system can definitively conclude that the model's underlying capability distribution has shifted, triggering an automatic suspension for human review.Human-Gated and Excluded UpdatesCertain facts must never update automatically. If a model demonstrates a severe safety violation, hallucinations on mission-critical workflows, or breaches a privacy boundary, transitioning the capability from "Active" to "Banned" in the Capability Registry requires human review. The automated system can open a semantic circuit breaker, zeroing out its routing weight temporarily, but permanent alteration requires an orchestrator's verification.Furthermore, the system must strictly ignore feedback that conflates user error with capability failure. If execution telemetry indicates the user provided a broken URL or a prompt contradicting the requested task shape, the resulting failure is classified as an Instruction Failure. The Capability Registry's rating for that model remains entirely untouched. A bad prompt is never a model failure.Practical Evaluation HierarchyEvaluating the quality of an AI-generated output is notoriously difficult. A provider's official documentation may claim support for a 128k context window, while the system's operational telemetry observes silent truncation at 64k. A model may score flawlessly on python script generation but fail catastrophically on database query generation. To handle these contradictions safely, the architecture utilizes Dempster-Shafer Theory and random-effects meta-analysis.Dempster-Shafer Theory for Evidence FusionTraditional Bayesian probability struggles with states of pure ignorance and direct evidentiary conflict. Dempster-Shafer (D-S) Theory introduces the concept of "Basic Belief Assignment" (BBA), allowing the system to assign probability mass not just to "True" or "False", but to the union of propositions representing "Conflict" and "Unknown".If the Capability Registry holds a high official claim (e.g., Context = 128k) but telemetry reports repeated failures at 70k, D-S Theory does not merely average the two into a mediocre "99k" belief. Instead, it measures the Jousselme distance between the evidence sources, detecting a high degree of mathematical conflict. When conflict exceeds a safety threshold, the system flags the capability attribute as "Contradictory". The router is then forced to act conservatively, utilizing the lower observed bound as a hard restriction until the Research Engine resolves the contradiction via targeted, bounded experiments.Meta-Analysis HeterogeneityWhen a capability performs well on one task type and poorly on another closely related task, averaging the outcomes destroys valuable routing signal. Using the Cochrane $Q$ statistic and the $I^2$ index from random-effects meta-analysis, the system can detect excessive heterogeneity in a model's outcome history. An $I^2$ value greater than $75\%$ mathematically proves that the variation in the model's performance is due to genuine heterogeneity rather than mere sampling error.When high heterogeneity is detected, the Research Engine intervenes to split the capability record. It ceases to track "Model Fit" globally for that domain and instead tracks "Model Fit (Python)" separately from "Model Fit (SQL)". This preserves prior evidence while correcting course, acknowledging that the model acts as a collection of narrow competencies rather than a monolithic intelligence.Practical ExamplesThe theoretical architecture translates into highly deterministic behaviors during real-world execution.Example 1: Fast, cheap, but poor semantic result on a difficult taskScenario: A local small-parameter model runs quickly and cheaply but produces a hallucinated result on a complex reasoning task.What OCC Records: The fast execution time, the low cost, and the poor evaluation score.
