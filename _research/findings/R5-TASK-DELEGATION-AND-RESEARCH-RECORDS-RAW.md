OCC Research Run R5: Task, Delegation, and Research RecordsFounder BriefThe Overseer Command Center (OCC) operates as a unified, provider-neutral AI orchestration application tasked with bridging deterministically routed operational work and continuous, exploratory research. The core architectural challenge facing OCC is the inherent risk of systemic divergence. OCC contains two distinct areas of work: the Router, which executes and schedules bounded tasks, and the Research Engine, which manages long-term objectives, hypotheses, and evidence gathering. The foundational risk is the accidental creation of a parallel execution environment. If the Research Engine begins independently scheduling agents, evaluating capabilities, or tracking execution costs, OCC will fracture into two competing command centers, destroying the integrity of its operational telemetry and cost accounting.To prevent this divergence, the architecture requires a strictly defined shared record model operating over an immutable event graph. The Router acts as the exclusive execution engine, managing the physical reality of the system: "how" a task is performed, "when" it is scheduled, and "what" it costs. The Research Engine acts as the epistemic navigator, managing the cognitive reality of the system: "why" work is requested, "what" hypotheses are being tested, and "what" evidence supports a potential architectural decision. When the Research Engine formulates a hypothesis requiring verification, it cannot execute work directly; it must translate that requirement into a bounded task and submit it to the Router for scheduling and capability assignment.This shared topology relies on treating all work as an immutable event history. Drawing inspiration from the Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) structures of version control systems and the distributed event histories of advanced workflow engines, OCC must append state changes as new versions rather than mutating past records. This ensures that queue views, cost tracking, and visual graph presentations are derived from the actual mathematical reality of the system's execution history, eliminating the need to invent post-hoc links to satisfy the user interface.Furthermore, integrating an evidence-to-decision framework ensures that an output generated by a routed AI model does not automatically become an accepted architectural truth. A raw model output is merely a source artifact. It must be subjected to formal grading, checked for contradictions against existing knowledge bases, and evaluated for certainty before it is elevated to accepted evidence. This separation of generation from ratification protects the system from hallucination-driven feedback loops.The proposed shared record model defines a lean set of core primitives that support both immediate operational routing and sophisticated research workflows. By adopting this shared graph, OCC maintains a single source of truth for all resource consumption, execution history, and capability tracking, establishing a foundation that is highly auditable and prepared for future scaling without vendor lock-in or premature storage commitments.1. Clear Ownership BoundariesA fundamental risk in multi-agent orchestration is the duplication of control systems. If the Research Engine builds its own capability registry, establishes its own task scheduler, or tracks its own API costs, OCC will suffer from divergent state management, conflicting resource allocation, and fragmented execution histories. To prevent this, OCC must enforce a strict, impermeable boundary between epistemic management (the Research Engine) and operational execution (the Router). This separation is analogous to the Incident Command System (ICS), where the Planning Section determines what objectives must be met, but the Operations Section directly manages the tactical deployment of resources.The Router is the exclusive execution engine. It owns the physical and operational reality of the system. The Router is solely responsible for determining the specific capability from the registry that is assigned, the moment the task is scheduled, the application of hard-rule safety gates, and the tracking of financial expenditure. The Router manages all state transitions related to work. Whether a task is queued, running, deferred to off-peak hours, blocked by a dependency, or waiting for human approval, the Router controls the state machine. The Router's pipeline—moving from classification to capability selection, cost estimation, scheduling, and outcome logging—is an exclusive operational domain that no other component is permitted to bypass.The Research Engine is the epistemic navigator. It owns the cognitive reality of the system. It is responsible for defining what needs to be learned, formulating hypotheses, grading the relevance of outputs, mapping contradictions, and preparing decision-ready handoffs for architectural or operational changes. The Research Engine operates entirely in the realm of knowledge, propositions, and evidence tracking. It does not know the current API price of a specific model, nor does it care whether a user is currently present to review a prompt. Its sole function is to evaluate information against defined goals.Both systems use the underlying record graph together. The Research Engine views Output Artifacts generated by the Router to grade them as Evidence Items. Conversely, the Router views the Research Engine's accepted findings as context for subsequent implementation tasks. Both systems rely on the same structured capability registry to understand what is computationally possible, and both contribute to the identical holistic visualization graph for the user interface.When the Research Engine needs to test a hypothesis or gather market intelligence, it must cross this ownership boundary through a highly specific protocol. It cannot execute an agent directly. Instead, the Research Engine formulates a bounded research request and submits it to the Router as a standard User Task. The Research Engine acts as an internal client to the Router. The Router applies its standard classifier, evaluates the five routing factors (Cost, Time/urgency, User presence, Model fit, Output quality tier), checks for required tool access, and executes the work. The Router maintains total sovereignty over the execution phase.Once the Router completes the task, it deposits an Output Artifact. The Research Engine then ingests this artifact across the boundary. Research results do not automatically become approved product direction. Inspired by the GRADE methodology, the Research Engine evaluates the Output Artifact for certainty, bias, and consistency. If the artifact meets the required confidence threshold, it is reclassified as an Evidence Item. If a collection of Evidence Items resolves a hypothesis, the Research Engine bundles them into a Decision Handoff, which is presented to MainOrch. MainOrch then ratifies the decision, triggering the creation of new operational implementation tasks back in the Router. This cycle ensures that learning and execution remain tightly coupled without ever violating their respective domains.3. Real Relationships for Graph and Queue ViewsOCC's user interface, including its queue, Mind Map View, and future Presentation Mode, must function as a direct projection of the underlying data structures. Constructing fake, post-hoc visual links degrades system trust and renders complex debugging impossible. The relationships between records must form a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG), similar to the Git object model or Temporal's distributed event history, ensuring total mathematical and chronological consistency.OCC must preserve explicit topological relationships to ensure the graph accurately reflects operational reality. When a complex User Task is submitted, the Router may split it into a parent-child relationship. The parent task cannot achieve a completed state until all child tasks resolve successfully. If Task B depends on the data generated by Task A, a Dependency relationship is formed. Task B remains in a blocked state until Task A is routed, executed, and yields an acceptable Output Artifact.The relationship between a User Task and its delegated execution runs is critical for understanding system friction. A single Task may spawn multiple Delegations if the initial attempt fails or times out. This relationship tracks retries and escalating costs, preventing a scenario where three failed attempts are silently erased, giving the false impression of flawless execution. Furthermore, if a Delegation reaches a hard-rule gate that requires human intervention—such as modifying a local file or exceeding a budget threshold—an Approval Record is created. The Delegation is suspended, and explicitly linked to the Approval Record, until a terminal state (approved or rejected) is registered.Once a Delegation completes, it is permanently linked to its exact Output Artifact. This link is secured by a cryptographic hash of the output's content, mirroring Git's SHA-1 Blob architecture, to guarantee that the record of what the model produced remains entirely immutable. In the epistemic layer, Research Objectives link to the specific scheduled Research Runs designed to test them. Research Runs link down to the Evidence Items they generated.When Evidence Item X directly opposes Evidence Item Y, a Contradiction record bridges them. This physical connection in the graph prevents either item from being blindly accepted and visually highlights unresolved tension in the system's epistemic state. Validated Evidence Items are subsequently linked to the specific Experiments that yielded them, and aggregate findings are mapped directly to Decision Handoffs. Once MainOrch ratifies a decision, any resulting operational tasks are linked back to the Decision Handoff, establishing full end-to-end traceability from a localized code deployment back to the original overarching research hypothesis.A strict distinction must be enforced between creation-time linking and post-hoc linking. Execution relationships—such as a Task spawning a Delegation, a Delegation requiring an Approval, or a Delegation generating an Output—must be rigidly recorded at the exact moment of creation. They represent immutable physical events in the system's history. Conversely, epistemic relationships are inherently post-hoc. An Output Artifact physically exists in the database before the Research Engine realizes it contradicts prior evidence or successfully supports a hypothesis. Therefore, epistemic links (Output to Evidence, Evidence to Contradiction) can be added later, provided they generate an evidence trail detailing precisely when, and by what logic, the link was established.4. Provenance, Version History, and TrustIn an AI-orchestrated environment, treating model outputs as implicit truths introduces critical vulnerabilities. Large language models hallucinate, capabilities degrade, and API responses shift over time. OCC must implement a robust provenance model to guarantee accountability. This model is heavily influenced by the W3C PROV-DM specification, which tracks the origin and processing history of every artifact by mapping the relationships between Entities (data), Activities (processes), and Agents (models or humans).OCC must strictly preserve the difference between the states of an artifact throughout its lifecycle. A Raw Research Result is merely an Output Artifact. It represents exactly what a specific capability generated at a specific microsecond. It carries zero epistemic weight and is not considered truth. An Audit of that Result represents a subsequent activity—either a human review or a secondary evaluator model pass—that evaluates the Output Artifact against predefined grading criteria, heavily mirroring the GRADE framework used in medical guidelines.Only after an Output Artifact has passed this audit and achieved a defined confidence score does it become an Accepted Research Finding, reclassified by the Research Engine as an Evidence Item. A collection of Evidence Items can be synthesized into a Proposed Decision, which is a structural handoff waiting for MainOrch review. Once MainOrch explicitly approves the handoff, it becomes a Ratified Decision, serving as an official architectural or operational directive for the system. If new data subsequently invalidates a Ratified Decision, the original record is not deleted or overwritten. Instead, a new Contradiction is logged, and a superseding Decision Handoff is generated, creating a permanent, traceable chain of correction and reversal.To maintain trust, OCC's interface and underlying data structures must explicitly demonstrate provenance at every node. Every Output Artifact must be explicitly linked to the exact Agent (the specific model version or tool) and the Activity (the precise Delegation ID) that generated it. Evidence Items must maintain clear downward links to their originating Source Artifacts, ensuring that a high-level architectural claim can always be traced back to the raw telemetry or external document that inspired it.If a task fact changes during the lifecycle of a request—such as a user escalating the urgency factor, or an environmental dependency failing—the system does not overwrite the original classification. Borrowing from the Git object model, records are immutable. A new version of the task fact record is appended to the event history, capturing exactly what changed, when it changed, and why it changed. State changes must include a standardized rationale code (e.g., "User escalated urgency," "API rate limit reached").Furthermore, the system must visually and structurally represent uncertainty. The Research Engine must tag Evidence Items with confidence ratings, ensuring that marginal data is not treated identically to extensively verified data. Unknown variables must be explicitly mapped as open Hypotheses. When conflicting data arises, the visual graph must highlight Contradiction records, physically connecting conflicting Evidence Items to demonstrate that the system recognizes its own uncertainty and refuses to proceed without further experimentation.5. Four Practical ExamplesTo demonstrate how the shared record model manages both determinism and flexibility, the following scenarios trace the exact lifecycle of records and their relational links.Scenario A: A Simple Normal Task (No Research)A user submits a prompt requesting a summary of a localized meeting transcript. The Router immediately generates a User Task record, classifying the request as low-cost, low-urgency, and requiring local file access but no human approval. Because the task is ready for execution, the Router evaluates the Capability Registry against the five routing factors, selecting a fast, local model. It generates a Delegation record and a Capability Assignment record to document this specific attempt and the rationale behind the selection. The assigned model processes the transcript. Upon completion, the system generates an Output Artifact containing the summary text and a cryptographic hash of the payload. The Delegation state transitions to complete. The User Task state transitions to resolved, establishing a permanent, unalterable link to the final Output Artifact.Scenario B: A Bounded Research Run (Like R1, R2, or R3)The Research Engine initiates an investigation into deterministic routing parameters. It creates a Research Objective ("Determine optimal routing gate order") and formulates a specific Hypothesis ("Hard restrictions must precede weighted scoring for accurate cost calculation"). To test this, the Research Engine formulates a User Task representing a detailed architectural audit and submits it across the boundary to the Router. The Router processes this identically to normal work, creating a Delegation and assigning a high-reasoning model capability. The model generates a comprehensive analysis, saved as an Output Artifact. The Research Engine evaluates the artifact. Finding the logic sound and the citations accurate, it grades the artifact, promoting it to an Evidence Item linked to the original Hypothesis. Because the evidence satisfies the objective, the Research Engine generates a Decision Handoff proposing the architecture update. MainOrch reviews the handoff and ratifies it, transforming it into a Ratified Decision.Scenario C: A Normal Task Depending on an Accepted Research FindingA user requests that OCC configure the new capability registry data structures. The Router creates a User Task. During the classification and context-gathering phase, the system identifies that this specific task relates directly to a prior Ratified Decision (the R2 Capability Registry Evidence audit). The Router prepares a Delegation. Crucially, it explicitly links the Ratified Decision as an input Source Artifact to the Delegation. When the assigned model begins execution, the architectural constraints defined in the ratified research are injected into the context window. The model generates the configuration code as an Output Artifact, ensuring total compliance with the established, evidence-backed research directives.Scenario D: A Multi-Step Task (Blocked, Approval Needed, Failed, Rerouted)A user requests a deployment of a destructive database schema update. The Router evaluates the complexity and splits the request into two User Tasks: Task 1 (Generate SQL) and Task 2 (Execute SQL). A Dependency record is generated, explicitly linking Task 2 as blocked by Task 1. The Router generates a Delegation for Task 1, which successfully executes and yields a SQL Output Artifact. The Dependency is resolved. The Router attempts to schedule Task 2, but the hard-rule gates detect a highly sensitive, irreversible action. The Router suspends the Delegation and spawns an Approval Gate record, placing the task in a "Waiting for User" state. The user reviews the SQL and approves the action. The Delegation transitions to running, but the database connection times out, resulting in a failure. The Router logs the failure, preserving the cost and exact API error. The Router then generates a second Delegation (a retry attempt), waiting for network health to restore. The second attempt succeeds, generating a final Output Artifact. The complete chain—Task generation, sequential dependency, manual authorization, execution failure, and successful retry—is permanently preserved in the event history graph.6. What Should Be Kept Simple At FirstTo support immediate internal dogfooding and prevent the architecture from collapsing under premature optimization, the initial record set must focus exclusively on operational stability. Advanced epistemic tracking can be layered over the core execution engine in later phases.The absolute minimum viable record set required for OCC to function truthfully includes the User Task (to define the work), the Delegation (to track the execution attempt), the Capability Assignment (to track the assigned agent and pricing), the Dependency / Blocker (for basic execution sequencing), the Approval Gate (for safety), and the Output Artifact (to capture the result).With only these six primitives, OCC can truthfully support a robust queue simply by querying the state of all current Delegation records. It can manage approval states by pausing delegations linked to pending Approval Gate records. It can execute accurate cost tracking by multiplying the token metrics stored in the Output Artifact against the pricing data preserved in the Capability Assignment. It can display real graph relationships by mapping the explicit parent, child, and dependency IDs between Tasks and Outputs, ensuring visual representations are mathematically genuine.Advanced research management features—such as formal Research Objectives, Hypotheses, Contradictions, and automated Decision Handoffs—can wait. During the initial build phase, internal Research Engine dogfooding can be managed by treating research queries as standard User Tasks and storing the results as standard Output Artifacts. Human operators can temporarily shoulder the burden of evaluating these artifacts and manually enforcing architectural decisions until the automated epistemic grading layer is fully constructed.7. Decision-Ready ConclusionsBased on the architectural directives, constraints, and the analysis of highly functional adjacent systems, the following conclusions establish the immediate developmental boundaries and required governance decisions for OCC.What OCC Cannot Truthfully Support WithoutOCC cannot truthfully support auditable cost tracking, deterministic replay, or real graph views without adopting an append-only event log combined with content-addressed artifacts. Any reliance on mutating databases or post-hoc relationship linking will immediately corrupt the system's operational trustworthiness. Furthermore, OCC cannot prevent systemic divergence without enforcing strict ownership boundaries. The Research Engine cannot generate execution logic; it must exclusively pass work requests to the Router as bounded User Tasks.What Can WaitAdvanced epistemic records—specifically the formal deployment of Contradictions, parameterized Hypotheses, and automated Decision Handoff grading—can wait. The initial build must focus relentlessly on stabilizing the Router's core execution loop. Additionally, advanced automated semantic circuit breakers for health monitoring of models remain a future research topic. The initial Capability Assignment only needs to track static metadata, hard constraints, and API pricing.What Remains UnprovenThe exact mathematical formula for balancing the five routing factors (Cost, Time, User presence, Model fit, Quality) remains entirely theoretical. It requires empirical testing against real-world execution data to ensure it aligns with human intuition. Additionally, while JSON/in-memory is the approved immediate storage direction, it remains unproven whether this architecture will scale efficiently when querying thousands of historical execution DAGs for complex contextual injection.What Needs MainOrch ReviewMainOrch must formally review and ratify the "split gates" concept proposed in R3. Moving hard restrictions (tool access, privacy boundaries) before weighted scoring, and reserving final verifications (human approval, live availability) for after scoring, fundamentally alters the routing pipeline. MainOrch must officially ratify this modification so the Delegation record schema can explicitly track which specific gate caused a routing failure. MainOrch must also formally accept the foundational architectural division of labor: the Router exclusively owns state transitions, and the Research Engine exclusively owns knowledge transitions.What Should Feed R4 (Outcome Feedback and Safe Improvement)Research run R4 must investigate exactly how to safely extract capability updates from completed tasks. It should adapt the GRADE methodology to score an AI model's historical performance, ensuring the system avoids falsely elevating a single lucky execution or a single API failure into an absolute truth about a capability. Furthermore, R4 must define exactly how the Capability Registry is updated based on the PROV-DM entity relationships established in this report. If an Output Artifact repeatedly fails human or automated audits, R4 must determine how those failures dynamically alter the responsible Agent's standing in the routing registry.
